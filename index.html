History of Semiconductors

From germanium transistors to nanometer nodes, this timeline explores the pivotal moments that shaped modern electronics.

1947: The First Transistor

Invented at Bell Labs by Bardeen, Brattain, and Shockley, the point-contact transistor marked the beginning of solid-state electronics.

1958: The Integrated Circuit

Jack Kilby at Texas Instruments created the first IC, reducing circuit size and launching the microelectronics era.

1971: Intel 4004

The world‚Äôs first commercial microprocessor revolutionized computing. It packed 2,300 transistors in a 4-bit CPU.

Leading-Edge Node Roadmap

3nm Process

2022-2024

TSMC achieves high yield, with Samsung competing using GAA transistors. This node powers current flagship devices.

2nm (N2) Process

Late 2025 - Early 2026

TSMC and Samsung target high-volume manufacturing with Gate-All-Around (GAA) transistors becoming the standard.

1.8nm (Intel 18A)

Late 2025 - 2026

Intel's major comeback effort, introducing backside power delivery and RibbonFET (its GAA version) for significant performance gains.

1.4nm & Beyond

2027+

The Angstrom era begins. Intel targets 14A with High-NA EUV lithography, while Samsung's 1.4nm plans are in development for post-2028.

Neuromorphic Computing

Mimicking the human brain to unlock ultra-efficient and adaptive computing systems.

Neuromorphic computing reimagines semiconductor design by mimicking how the human brain processes information‚Äîusing spikes, parallelism, and event-driven logic. Unlike traditional von Neumann architectures, neuromorphic systems integrate memory and processing in each unit, enabling massive efficiency in edge AI applications.

This paradigm is especially promising for real-time processing in robotics, autonomous systems, prosthetics, and energy-constrained environments like wearables or satellites.
